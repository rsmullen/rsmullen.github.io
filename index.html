About Me

I am a postdoc in the Center for Theoretical Astrophysics and CCS-2 at Los Alamos National Laboratory in Los Alamos, New Mexico, where I collaborate with Chris Fryer and Jenn Donley. I received my PhD in 2020 from the University of Arizona where I worked with the amazing Dr. Kaitlin Kratter.

All of my wide-ranging research interests—from Pluto to planetary dynamics to star formation—stem from a long-lived fascination with binaries. I enjoy applying new and different techniques to data to help us extract the most information possible. Currently, I'm exploring how we can learn more about the evolution of dense cores in simulations using machine learning and computer vision.

Teaching and Outreach

I have always been an advocate for science communication and outreach. Astronomy, in my opinion, is the best "gateway science". The night sky and its contents are a shared experience across language, culture, and education. Therefore, I strive to make astronomy interesting and accessible to everyone. I am especially passionate about (re)igniting an interest in astronomy, physics, and math for teens and adults.

Research

I use a variety of computational methods to study many topics surrounding star and planet formation and evolution.

The time evolution of star forming cores

We study how star forming cores identified with dendrograms vary in time.

Learn more

The architecture of circumbinary systems

We study the impact of a central binary on the dynamical evolution of planetary systems.

Learn more

The fate of debris around the Pluto-Charon system

We study the outcomes of debris scattering after the formation of the Pluto-Charon binary.

Learn more

Machine learning classification of Kuiper belt populations

We classify observed Kuiper Belt objects with a machine learning algorithm to prepare for the VRO.
